This isn't a normal vacation. It's five days designed around one idea: pack in everything, waste nothing, make the kind of memories that stick for decades.

We start in Cincinnati — a museum on the border between slavery and freedom, dinner at a top-100 restaurant, and a sunset from 85 stone steps above the Ohio River. Then we wake up before the sun and drive into Kentucky horse country — Thoroughbreds thundering past at 40 mph, breakfast with jockeys, hand-feeding baby foals, and railbikes through the Bluegrass. Then Louisville — Kentucky's most famous dish, Oaks Day under the lights, and the Derby itself in premium seats.

Day 1 · Wednesday, April 29

Cincinnati

The Earned View
We land at 2:48 PM and hit the ground running. By 9 PM we'll have stood on the banks of the Ohio River where enslaved people crossed to freedom, eaten housemade pasta at a top-100 restaurant, climbed 85 stone steps to the highest point in Cincinnati, and watched the entire skyline light up beneath us.
Rain plan: Freedom Center, dinner, and Graeter's are all indoors — no changes needed. If it's raining, skip Mt. Adams (stone steps get slippery, no sunset to see). Instead, spend more time exploring OTR after dinner — Rhinegeist Brewery (1910 Elm St, massive taproom — root beer for you, something stronger for the dads) or the OTR Candy Bar (vintage soda floats).
2:48 PM
Land at CVG Airport
›

4:15 PM
National Underground Railroad Freedom Center
›
This museum sits on the banks of the Ohio River — the actual, physical line that separated slave states from free states. Enslaved people crossed this exact stretch of water, often at night, risking everything. We have 45 minutes before it closes at 5 PM — this is a focused visit. Head straight to the 2nd floor slave pen — an actual structure from a Kentucky farm where human beings were held, moved piece by piece into the museum. It's the most powerful piece and anchors everything else. Then walk through the Underground Railroad gallery on the same floor. These two stops alone will change the way you see the Ohio River for the rest of the trip.
📍 Google Maps · Reviews & Hours 🌐 freedomcenter.org
5:00 PM
Explore Over-the-Rhine
›
Drive five minutes north to OTR — the largest collection of Italianate architecture in the country. Street art, murals, restored buildings, boutiques, and the hum of a neighborhood that came back from the dead. Walk Vine Street and Main Street. There's a concentrated stretch of murals along Main Street between 12th and 14th that's worth seeking out. We have about an hour before dinner.

Dinner — Pepp & Dolores

The name comes from the owners' Italian grandparents, and the whole place feels like it — warm, loud, familial. Wednesday hours are 4–10 PM. The Sunday Sauce (spaghetti with meatball and braised pork, $19) is the signature. The spicy vodka mezze rigatoni ($17) is what you'll fight over. The lobster squid-ink pasta ($25) is Danny's move.

Mt. Adams & The Immaculata Steps
›
This is the moment that makes Day 1 unforgettable. 85 stone steps climbing straight up to a limestone church built in 1859, perched on the highest point in Cincinnati. Climb. Every. Step. At the top, turn around. The entire Ohio River valley opens up — the Cincinnati skyline glowing in the last light, the river curving south into Kentucky, the bridges lit up like jewels. Sunset on April 29 is approximately 8:35 PM. Aim to reach the top by 8:15 for the golden-to-blue-hour transition. Bring your phone for photos but then put it away for a minute and just look.

Day 2 · Thursday, April 30

Horse Country

Before the World Wakes Up
This is the day that separates this trip from anything you've done before. Thoroughbreds thundering past close enough to feel the air move. Three private horse farms where you'll hand-feed baby foals. Railbikes through the Bluegrass to a bridge 283 feet above the Kentucky River. The most beautiful drive in the eastern United States. Yes, we're waking up at 5:30 AM. Trust the process.
Thurby note: Churchill Downs has Thursday racing (gates open 10:30 AM) — it's called Thurby and Louisville locals love it — but we're skipping it for horse country. What we'll see today is something you can't get at the track.
Rain plan: Horse country works in rain! Keeneland workouts run unless lightning. Farm tours run rain or shine (wear shoes that can handle mud). Rail Explorers runs in light rain but cancels for thunderstorms — call 877-833-8588 if weather is rough. Old Frankfort Pike is actually gorgeous in the mist. Brown Hotel, Big Four Bridge, and NuLu are all rain-proof. Just bring a light rain jacket.
5:30 AM
Wake Up & Go
›
No sit-down breakfast. Grab whatever the hotel has for grab-and-go — coffee for the dads, a muffin for the car — and get on the road. We're eating breakfast at a place that's worth the drive.
7:15 AM
Keeneland — Morning Workouts
›
Pull into Keeneland through Gate 2. Walk to the rail. In the soft morning light, Thoroughbred racehorses come thundering past at 40 miles an hour. They run in pairs, jockeys crouched low, hooves shaking the ground. Steam rises off their backs in the cool April air. The track takes a renovation break at 7:00–7:30, then the fastest horses run speed work right after. That 20-minute post-break window is the best part of the morning. FREE, no reservation, open to the public.
📍 Google Maps · Reviews & Hours 🌐 keeneland.com
7:50 AM
Track Kitchen — Quick Breakfast
›
A cafeteria inside the track where jockeys, trainers, exercise riders, and horse owners eat breakfast. The person at the table next to you might be the jockey who rode a Derby winner. The food is simple, cheap, and made for people who've been working since 4:30 AM. We've got 15 minutes — biscuits and gravy to go — because we have a farm tour to catch.
8:30 AM
Private Horse Farm Tours
›
For 2.5–3 hours, we visit three working Thoroughbred breeding and training farms normally closed to the public. Barns where stallions worth millions live. Paddocks where mares graze with newborn foals. Alexis will hand-feed a three-week-old foal peppermints. She'll stroke a mare's nose while the guide tells the story of that horse's bloodline — maybe she's the daughter of a Derby winner. The smell of hay, the warmth of a horse's breath, the sound of foals playing — this is the kind of sensory memory that stays forever.
📍 Google Maps · Reviews & Hours 🌐 seethechampions.com
1:00 PM
Rail Explorers — Railbikes
›
The afternoon's secret weapon. Pedal a four-person railbike on actual train tracks through Bluegrass horse farms and limestone cliffs. The turnaround is near Young's High Bridge — 283 feet above the Kentucky River. Electric-assist motors make the pedaling easy. This is a perspective on horse country you literally cannot get any other way: gliding silently through private farmland on century-old rails. $199 for a Quad bike (4 riders). 1:00 PM departure from Bluegrass Museum. Thursday departures also available at 10:30 AM and 3:30 PM.
📍 Google Maps · Reviews & Hours 🎫 Book Now 🌐 railexplorers.net
2:30 PM
Wallace Station — Late Lunch
›
A beloved sandwich shop on Old Frankfort Pike belonging to Chef Ouita Michel — an eight-time James Beard semifinalist. Racing silks and farm logos on the walls. Baked goods from Weisenberger Mill flour (since 1865). Horse farm workers and trainers eat here. The ham and swiss on house bread is the sleeper hit. The cookies are massive.
📍 Google Maps · Reviews & Hours 🌐 wallacestation.com
3:00 PM
Old Frankfort Pike Scenic Drive
›
Rolling Bluegrass hills. Dry-laid limestone fences that have stood for two centuries. White-board horse fences stretching to the horizon. Legendary farms — Three Chimneys, Darby Dan, Donamire — where Derby winners were born. A stretch where ancient trees form a cathedral canopy over the road. At 3 PM the light has that honeyed quality that makes Kentucky horse country look like a painting. Roll the windows down. Go slow. Stop often.
3:30 PM
Midway, Kentucky
›
One street. Railroad tracks down the middle. Independent shops, galleries, and restaurants in restored 19th-century buildings. Horses grazing in fields at the edge of town. 20–30 minutes to walk the street.

5:30 PM
Arrive Louisville
›
Welcome to Derby City. We skip the Airbnb and head straight to dinner. After a day that started at 5:30 AM at a racetrack, everyone deserves to sit down in a beautiful room and eat the most famous dish in Kentucky.
6:00 PM
The Brown Hotel — The Hot Brown
›
This is a pilgrimage. In 1926, Chef Fred Schmidt created an open-faced turkey sandwich smothered in Mornay sauce, topped with bacon and tomato, then broiled until bubbling. A hundred years later, it's Kentucky's most famous dish, and this is the only place to eat the original. The room is gorgeous — dark wood, brass, old-hotel elegance that makes you sit up straighter. Business casual. Danny will order two. There's a $100/person food & beverage minimum — so go big. Danny already booked this one: 6:00 PM Thursday.
📍 Google Maps · Reviews & Hours 🌐 brownhotel.com

Big Four Bridge

A converted railroad bridge spanning the Ohio River, beautifully lit at night with a nightly light show. Walk across — the river is wide and dark below, the Louisville skyline glows behind you. On the Indiana side, a spiral ramp descends into a park. The perfect post-dinner walk. ~30 min round trip. Free.

Day 3 · Friday, May 1

Kentucky Oaks

The Locals' Derby — PINK OUT
Many Louisville locals prefer Oaks Day to the Derby itself. The Kentucky Oaks is the premier race for three-year-old fillies, it draws 100,000+ fans, and the atmosphere is electric but more local and more approachable than Saturday. Friday is the Pink Out — the entire crowd wears pink for breast cancer awareness. Big 2026 news: NBC is broadcasting the Oaks in primetime — CONFIRMED for 8:00 PM on NBC. The race will run under the lights. We're witnessing history.
Dress code: PINK! Everyone wears pink for the Pink Out. Hats, sundresses, the works. Comfortable shoes — you'll be on your feet 10+ hours. Bring backup flats. No umbrellas allowed — clear poncho if rain threatens.
Churchill Downs 2026 renovation: Major new spaces this year — the new Conservatory, Pagoda Club & Terrace, Stargazer Lounge, and renovated Finishing Line Suites. Check your Derby Experiences package for access to these new spaces.

Day 5 · Sunday, May 3

The Long Goodbye

Louisville → Home
We went to the Kentucky Derby last night. Nobody is waking up early. Sleep until you wake up. Pack slowly. We don't fly out of CVG until 8:00 PM — so we have a full day to say goodbye to Louisville. Two options below depending on whether we want to hit the American Sign Museum on the way to the airport.
10:00 AM
Louisville Slugger Museum
›
A 120-foot steel baseball bat leaning against the building. Inside: the actual production line where MLB bats are turned on lathes. The vault of player specs (every MLB player has their own bat profile). Everyone gets a free mini bat. Danny will be in heaven. Factory tours don't run Sundays but the museum, batting cages, and Babe Ruth gallery are still excellent. 60–75 min.
📍 Google Maps · Reviews & Hours 🌐 sluggermuseum.com
11:00 AM
Muhammad Ali Center
›
Three floors on the life and legacy of Muhammad Ali, born right here in Louisville. 2026 marks the 10th anniversary of Ali's passing. A full-size boxing ring with projected footage. The exhibit on Ali's social justice work connects to the Freedom Center we visited Day 1. The "I Am Ali" immersive experience on the top floor is the highlight. 90 minutes. 5-minute walk from Slugger Museum.
📍 Google Maps · Reviews & Hours 🌐 alicenter.org
12:30 PM
NuLu Brunch & Exploring
›
Biscuit Belly — fried chicken on a biscuit with pimento cheese, roasted jalapeños, pickles, and a fried green tomato. Order the "Gravy Train" to try all three gravies. Or Lou Lou on Market — the whole New Orleans experience: Cajun-Creole, beignets, shrimp and grits, a basement speakeasy with live music. Or try NuLu Mashup Food Hall (750 E Jefferson St) — 7 eateries under one roof, everyone picks their own thing. Then explore NuLu properly. Boutiques. Street art. Vintage shops. Muth's Candies for Modjeskas.
